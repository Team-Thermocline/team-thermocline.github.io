# Final Report

Our final report

Abstract:
SNHU’s final capstone course required students to collaborate across engineering disciplines to
design, fabricate, test, analyze, optimize, and present a working product that satisfied customer
requirements. Each student brought their unique mechanical and electrical experience to help
develop the final product: a thermally-controlled PCB testing chamber that delivered efficient and
highly successful performance. Team Thermocline was originally presented with several driving
requirements provided by the customer – an electrical engineering professor, which described
product dimension, function, and performance criteria. From this, the group developed a
conceptual design, including materials, components, control features, and the expected assembly
integration process. Trade studies were then conducted across the project, supported by external
consultations with engineering, HVAC, and manufacturing/machining professionals to approve – or
improve – the product design. Initial studies included prototype electronic boards and mechanical
parts to test supply chain capabilities and electronic performance feasibility. The team built on
these initial steps by designing the mechanical structure, creating the main electronic control
board, sourcing thermal-fluid components, ordering critical pieces, and 3D-printing/laser-cutting
parts. Assembly was supplemented with iterative designing, prototyping, and functional testing of
individual subsystems. The end of the course brought performance testing to observe alignment
with customer requirements, and presenting to relevant project stakeholders. Proper
documentation was completed throughout the project, showing clear product development
history. By the end, all system and driving customer requirements were met, achieved by
persistent, insightful, and collaborative teamwork. However, one derived requirement was not met
(CON-05), describing the prohibition of thermal control when the user is handling the PCB. This
resulted from a lack of testing with physical PCBs inside the chamber, and implementing the
necessary sensors/measurements/controls to enforce this requirement. In spite of this, the team
successfully designed, built, and tested the thermally-controlled chamber that met/exceeded
most requirements, and served as an enlightening experience for developing well-rounded
engineers.
